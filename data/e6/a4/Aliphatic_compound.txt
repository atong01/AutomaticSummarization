In [[organic chemistry]], compounds composed of [[carbon]] and [[hydrogen]] are divided into two classes: [[aromatic]] compounds and '''aliphatic compounds''' ({{IPAc-en|ˌ|æ|l|ɨ|ˈ|f|æ|t|ɨ|k}}; G. ''aleiphar'', fat, oil) also known as non-aromatic compounds.   Aromatic compounds contain an aromatic-ring configuration of atoms, such as [[benzene]], whereas aliphatic compounds do not.<ref>{{GoldBookRef |title=Aliphatic compounds |file=A00217 |year=1995}}</ref>  Aliphatic compounds can be saturated, like [[hexane]], or unsaturated, like [[hexene]].

== Structure ==

Aliphatic compounds can be [[Saturation (chemistry)|saturated]], joined by single bonds ([[alkane]]s), or unsaturated, with double bonds ([[alkene]]s) or triple bonds ([[alkyne]]s). Besides [[hydrogen]], other elements can be bound to the carbon chain, the most common being [[oxygen]], [[nitrogen]], [[sulphur]], and [[chlorine]]. 

The simplest aliphatic compound is [[methane]] (CH<sub>4</sub>). Aliphatics include [[alkane]]s, [[alkene]]s, and [[alkyne]]s.

== Properties ==

Most aliphatic compounds are flammable, allowing the use of hydrocarbons as fuel, such as [[methane]] in Bunsen burners and as [[liquified natural gas]] (LNG), and [[acetylene]] in welding.

== Examples of aliphatic compounds / non-aromatic  ==

The most important aliphatic compounds are:

* n-, iso- and cyclo-alk'''an'''es (saturated hydrocarbons)
* n-, iso- and cyclo-alk'''en'''es and -alk'''yn'''es (unsaturated hydrocarbons).

==Aliphatic acids==
Aliphatic acids are the acids of nonaromatic hydrocarbons, such as [[acetic acid|acetic]], [[Propionic acid|propionic]], and [[Butyric acid|butyric]] acids.
